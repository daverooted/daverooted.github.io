The more you travel, the better at it you get. We’ve definitely learned a lot from each trip and, as a result, our trips run more smoothly each time we hit the road. Here are some of the things we’ve learned, so you can avoid making these mistakes on your next trip.

Pack light | Have a plan | Pace yourself | Walk

  1. Pack light. For our trip to Southeast Asia in 2007, we both bought huge backpacks with frames. They were both pretty packed when we first arrived. Ana prioritizes looking cute, which for her meant bringing a lot of options. Every time we relocated (which happened three or four times, I think), we had a lot to carry. This was particularly hard on Ana. And we didn’t anticipate all the gifts we wanted to buy, which we had to carry everywhere we went. It was a lot. We’ve since bought smaller backpacks and on our last trip, Ana opted for a small, rolling suitcase instead of her backpack. (She also balances practicality and style better now.) Someday, maybe we’ll even be able to get everything into one backpack for both of us. I usually bring about six days worth of clothes and then do laundry every five days or so. This means doing laundry once for a 10-day trip and about six times for a five week trip. I like finding laundromats and doing my laundry in different countries, because it feels like an authentic local experience that isn’t made for tourists. You get to see a side of the country you might not otherwise see. And you might meet people that way too.
  2. Have a plan. We’ve had detailed, carefully timed plans on some of our trips and rougher plans on others. But we always have a plan. That means doing our research ahead of time, using a combination of web searches, buying travel books, and talking to people we know who’ve been where we’re going. When it comes to web searches, one of our favorite sites is Atlas Obscura. It is a wealth of oddities. We’ve found some of the best places we’ve ever seen on Atlas Obscura. When it comes to books, our favorite books are written by Rick Steves. Unfortunately, he doesn’t have a guide for every country, but if he does, it’ll be well worth the money. His book about Italy saved us a lot of time and money. When you’re planning, prioritize what you want to see, but be aware of second choices that are nearby. Sometimes you’ll go somewhere you thought would be great and it turns out to be way overrated. Having back-up sites nearby is good planning.
  3. Pace yourself. When you start looking into all the amazing things you could see and do in a place, you’ll want to do way more than you’ll have time to do. Be realistic about how long things will take. For instance, if you’re going to St. Peter’s Basilica in Rome, go first thing in the morning (to beat the crowds) and block off the whole morning. You might think, it’s just a church, how long can it take to see a church? The truth is you could spend a day in a place like this. Give yourself time to really see everything. Be in a place long enough for the feeling of it to wash over you. Soak it all in. Also, be sure to build in time for meals, even if you’re packing a lunch or dinner in your day bag. It’s important to stop, sit, rest, and reflect periodically. You might have the energy to push yourself for the first few days, but at some point that will catch up with you and you’ll find yourself in a hotel in a faraway place.
  4. Walk. Most trips will involve a lot of mechanized travel: planes, boats, cars, tuk-tuks, etc. Use them when you must (like in transit to or from the airport), but don’t over-rely on them. Walk as much as you can. If you’re heading for the subway to go two stops, walking will often take not much longer. And you see things when you walk that you will never see from a vehicle. You can observe people, notice out-of-the-way passages and places, and stumble on great finds. In Munich once, we were walking down a street and noticed a cathedral. We walked in and it was one of the most amazing Baroque cathedrals either of us had ever seen. We walk a lot on our trips. In Prague, we walked an average of six miles a day. We definitely could have gotten around on buses or the metro (subway), which we did some, when the distances were far. But we loved walking through neighborhoods, seeing playgrounds and schools, apartment buildings, local shops. The exercise is also good for you; it helps keep your head clear and gives you a chance to talk to the people you’re traveling with. Hoof it as much as you can.
I hope these tips are helpful!
