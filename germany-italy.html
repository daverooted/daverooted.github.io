The other five-week trip we took was to Germany and Italy. We spent the first week in Germany with my brother and his family. They live in Nuremberg. We arranged for my mother to be there at the same time as we were there. A lot of what we did was things the kids would like, but we did spend a little time exploring the city, including the castle. It’s a pretty city with a complicated history. It was once the seat of the Holy Roman Empire. It was also Hitler’s favorite city. Germany has done a lot of work to ensure that someone like Hitler wouldn’t be able to again rise to power with so much popular support. Thanks in part to conditions of their surrender in 1945, they are today one of the most stable, prosperous countries in Europe. We will definitely be back to visit again, because we have family there. We always hope to catch a few more sights when we go.

After our week-long visit with family, we caught a sleeper train to Italy and woke up in one of my favorite cities in the world, Venice. It had been more than twenty years since I’d been, but it still looked exactly the same. I’m sure it will, until it sinks into the sea. My favorite cathedral in Europe is there: St. Mark’s. And as someone who loves to walk, Venice is a treat. Because of the canals, there are no cars or even bicycles. You get around on foot or by boat. It’s the best place to get lost.

After Venice, we headed down to Florence, which we used as our base of operations for about eight days. We of course took in the sights in Florence itself, like the Uffizi, Michelangelo’s David, the Ponte Vecchio, and one of many incredible anatomical museums we visited. We took jaunts from Florence to Siena, Bologna, Pisa, and Cinque Terre, one of the idyllic places I’ve ever been. We wished we’d had more time in Pisa, but we can always go back.

From Florence, we headed down to Naples, a city I’d never visited. We both loved it. I think it was Ana’s favorite. It somehow seemed more real, not overrun with so many non-Italian tourists. We spent a day on the island of Capri, which was slightly marred by Ana’s seasickness getting there. I rode a chair lift to the top of the island for an amazing view of the Mediterranean. And, like every other day we were in Italy, we ate lots of yummy gelato and sorbetto! Back in Naples, we hopped over to Pompeii for a day in the ruins under the shadow of Mount Vesuvius.

After Naples, we headed to Rome for our last week or so. We also springboarded a few day trips out of Rome, including one to Assisi and one to Ostia Antica. But we spent most of our time in the city—there’s just too much to see in a city with as much history as it has (are there cities with as much history as Rome?). We spent a day in the Vatican Museum. Wow. And we popped into so many cathedrals, including St. Peter’s and St. Paul’s. Really, you could spend an entire trip to Italy just visiting cathedrals and you would see more amazing art for free than you could imagine. In fact, some of the most famous works of Italian art can only be seen that way. We also visited the Forum and the Colosseum, as well as the Pantheon and some early Christian catacombs. And did I mention the delicious Italian food? We bought so many gifts for people that we had to buy an extra suitcase just to get it all home! We hope this was just our first trip to Italy.
