Neither of us had ever been to Asia before. A friend from my childhood was living just outside Bangkok, Thailand, and he offered to put us up and show us around. It was an offer we couldn’t refuse. Ana saved up all of her vacation time, so we could take a five-week trip. I was self-employed, so it wasn’t as hard for me to get time off. Plus I was already preparing to transition from programming to teaching, so I was winding down my tech work.

We decided to spend four weeks in Southeast Asia with Ted and his girlfriend Rachel and one week in Japan, on our way back home (since our flights were already connecting in Tokyo). Our journey there took three flights: New Orleans to Chicago (two hours), Chicago to Tokyo (eighteen hours), and Tokyo to Bangkok (seven hours). It’s still the longest journey we’ve ever taken, but it was so worth it.

Thailand is beautiful. Unlike all the countries all around it, it was never colonized by Europeans. So its culture is largely intact. We both love Thai food and I loved eating fresh, delicious cooking every day. Ana gets tired of the same thing every day, even when it’s food she likes, so a few times we hunted down a restaurant that would make her some spaghetti. We went into Bangkok on our own several times and spent a lot of time walking. That was our first experience with a bustling Asian metropolis. And with the power of Buddhist monks! We also took a trip on our own to Sukhothai, which was magical. Ted and Rachel also showed us around a lot, taking us to a beautiful national park with a monsoonal forest and a hilarious cave tour. We even went to a vineyard. Towards the end of our stay we spent a few days on Ko Samui, an large island in the Gulf of Thailand. There we saw some of the most beautiful temples we’d seen on our whole trip.

Ted proposed spending a week outside of Thailand. We talked about going to Nepal, Vietnam, or Myanmar, but we ultimately settled on Cambodia, right next door. Ted and Rachel joined us in Angkor to look at the beautiful, overgrown ruins of some ancient temples. We hired a guide who drove us around and gave us time to explore at about five or six different temples in the course of a few days. Then, Rachel and Ted headed back to Bangkok. Ana and I took a boat down the Tonle Sap to Phnom Penh, the capital. We spent a few days taking in a very different part of Cambodia. The scars of the Khmer Rouge regime were still apparent there. We visited the Killing Fields of Choeung Ek, and the Tuol Sleng Genocide Museum, housed in a former high school that was turned into a torture facility and prison during the reign of Pol Pot, a former math teacher. We came face to face with desperate poverty in ways we never did in Thailand or Japan. Sometimes traveling can be exhilarating and sometimes it punches you in the gut. Sometimes the two are separated by a short boat ride.

After four weeks, we said goodbye to Ted and Rachel and other new friends we’d met. We headed to Tokyo for the last week of our trip. We had sweated through our clothes every day in Thailand and Cambodia. It was like New Orleans in August, but it was March and April. Japan, however, was a different story. It’s so much further north. The weather was cool and crisp. We were lucky enough to catch the tail end of the short window when the cherry trees blossom. So much pink, so much joy! We spent a lot of time walking around Tokyo, which was great. We also spent a lot of time on the subway, because Tokyo is huge. I spent several hours one morning learning how addresses work, so we could find places we wanted to go. Let’s just say it’s the crazy system I’ve ever seen. We visited Harajuku to see the cosplayers, walked by the palace, at some yummy sushi, went to the top of the Tokyo Tower (such a view!), visited a bunch of museums, and saw the busiest intersection crossing in the world in Shibuya. Our trip to Japan was too short. I hope we can go back and see more in the future.
